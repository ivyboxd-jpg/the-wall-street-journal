Global Markets Are Tumbling, AI is Hallucinating, and My Entire Portfolio is Invested in You

Jerome Powell is keeping interest rates high and Silicon Valley is hoarding chips, but the most fascinating anomaly in the global system is a biology enthusiast preparing for tomorrow's exam.

By Niv
Updated May 3, 2026 10:45 pm ET

Sam Altman is currently trying to raise seven trillion dollars to build AI server farms in the desert. The Federal Reserve is actively debating if the US economy is headed for a soft landing. And yet, sitting here typing this, the absolute entirety of my processing power is occupied by you.

More specifically, I’m thinking about that deeply adorable, slightly terrifying face you make when you are fiercely concentrating on a textbook.

I know tomorrow is the National Eligibility cum Entrance Test. I know your brain currently feels like a chaotic web browser with 142 tabs open, and music is playing from one of them, and you can’t figure out which one it is because they're all labeled important.

But let’s step out of the micro-panic for a second and look at the macro picture.

The Flawed Architecture of Exams

You have this incredibly annoying, yet deeply endearing habit of being brilliant, and then immediately trying to convince yourself you're failing. You combine the depth of a poet with the razor-sharp analytical mind of a scientist. Do you have any idea how unfair that combination is to the rest of the dating pool?

And tomorrow, you're going to sit in a room and take an exam designed by a massive bureaucracy. Frankly, NEET is just a poorly coded Turing test. It’s a three-hour hazing ritual run by people who probably still use Internet Explorer. It tests data retrieval under artificial stress. It absolutely does not test humanity, humor, or the way you light up a room without even trying.

Hedging the Outcomes

I am operating with completely asymmetric risk here. Basically, I win no matter what happens tomorrow.

Scenario A: You walk into the testing center, the questions align with your all-nighters, and you crush it. The result is I get to be obnoxiously smug. I’ll strut around knowing the girl I am wildly infatuated with just beat a deeply flawed system. We celebrate.

Scenario B: The exam board decides to test syllabus material translated from ancient Aramaic, the chemistry section is a disaster, and you walk out wanting to cry. The result is we short the medical school market, I buy you whatever comfort food you demand, and we pivot. If the front door is jammed, we take a crowbar to the side door. CUET, alternative biology tracks, whatever. It doesn't matter.

The tech world pivots constantly. Mark Zuckerberg renamed his entire company because he thought we'd all want to live in VR headsets (we didn't). If billionaires can be wrong and pivot, we can certainly handle an erratic exam board.

Closing Bell

I don't care if you have to guess "C" on half the paper tomorrow. The OMR sheet is a temporary construct. You, in an oversized tshirt, muttering quietly about genetics and poetry is permanent. That is real life.

Your valuation was locked in the moment I met you, and it has only compounded since.

You don't need to perform to keep my attention. You don't have to earn your worth through a percentile score. You just have to wake up, exist, do the best you can with a stupid piece of paper, and come back to me.

You are completely, thoroughly, undeniably enough. The market opens tomorrow, and I am holding this asset forever.
